The math — every formula, and the choices we made (please check our work)

Why this is spelled out. This tool is free, shows no ads, and tracks nothing — and a spoke calculator is only worth using if you can trust it. Trust comes from being able to check it, not from being asked to. So every number below is defined, every modelling choice is stated plainly (including where we differ from other tools and where we're deliberately approximate), and the whole thing is one readable file: View Source, or read it on GitHub. If you find an error, that's a bug we want to fix.

Spoke length

L = √(R² + r² + f² − 2·R·r·cos θ) − ½·(flange spoke-hole dia)

  • R = ERD / 2 — the rim's effective radius, the point the spoke end should reach.
  • r = flange PCD / 2 — radius of the hub's spoke-hole circle.
  • f — axial centre-to-flange distance, adjusted by hub offset and drilling offset.
  • θ = crosses × 720 / spoke-count — angle at the axle between a hub hole and its rim hole.
  • We subtract half the hub flange hole (the spoke seats against the far side of the hole). This is a hub dimension — that's why it lives in the Hub panel.

Three choices a careful builder should know about

  • Spoke-stretch compensation (optional). By default we report the geometric length, like spocalc, DT and Sapim. Tick compensate and each spoke is shortened by the elongation it takes at your build tension — ΔL = T·L/(E·A), with E = 200 GPa and A = π·(gauge/2)² — giving the unstressed length to cut/order (Musson does this; ~0.2–0.5 mm, thinner spokes more). Area uses the spoke gauge as straight; butted spokes stretch a touch more.
  • Nipple length adjusts ERD. ERD depends on the nipple (~2 mm of effective ERD between a 12 and 16 mm nipple — a whole spoke size). We take the entered ERD as measured to a 12 mm nipple's slot and shift it by 2·(nipple − 12); the effective ERD is shown when it differs. Best practice: bench-measure ERD with the nipples you'll actually use and leave the field at 12.
  • Bench measurements beat catalogue specs. Manufacturer ERD especially is often optimistic. When precision matters, measure your own rim (two-spoke method) and hub.
  • Uncertainty is shown, not hidden. Set ERD ± to your measurement's confidence and we propagate it through the formula to a band on the length (e.g. 294.2 ±0.5). Picking a rim from the catalogue auto-sets ±1 mm, since those values are unverified; a bench-measured ERD deserves 0.

The other numbers

  • Bracing angle = atan(f / in-plane run) — the spoke's angle to the wheel plane; more is better lateral support.
  • Tension distribution — for the wheel to sit centred, T·sin(bracing) must match on both sides, so tension ∝ 1/sin(bracing). We pin the tighter side at 100% and show the other as its true ratio. The familiar "offset ratio" (e.g. 50%) is the small-angle approximation of the same thing.
  • Lever arm = perpendicular distance from the axle to the spoke (Musson's term). Effective tangential PCD = 2 × lever arm.
  • Flange exit angle = asin(lever-arm / r); rim entry angle = asin(lever-arm / R). The rim entry angle is what stresses the nipple and decides whether you want washers.

Head clearance (our model, not a standard)

clearance = √(gap² + flange_thickness²) − spoke_gauge, where gap is the in-plane perpendicular distance from the crossing neighbour's hole to the spoke line. Adjacent heads sit on opposite faces of the flange, so thickness genuinely adds room. No other calculator computes this the same way, so don't expect the number to match — but the hub-detail diagram draws it so you can judge it with your own eyes. For crow's foot it's an approximation (computed on the crossed spokes).

Lacing patterns

Length depends only on each spoke's θ, so a pattern is fully described by the set of crossings it uses. We support radial (0×) through 5-cross (one length), and crow's foot — a repeating radial + crossed group that yields two lengths and needs the spokes-per-side divisible by 3 (24/36/48h). Decorative names like snowflake are rearrangements that reduce to one of these for length purposes. Triplet (grouped hub holes) and straight-pull hubs change the hub geometry itself and aren't modelled yet — we'd rather omit them than ship a length we can't stand behind.

The math — every formula, and the choices we made (please check our work)

Why this is spelled out. This tool is free, shows no ads, and tracks nothing — and a spoke calculator is only worth using if you can trust it. Trust comes from being able to check it, not from being asked to. So every number below is defined, every modelling choice is stated plainly (including where we differ from other tools and where we're deliberately approximate), the math lives in one small file — spokecalc.js — with a test suite that cross-checks every length against an independent 3-D computation. View Source, or read it on GitHub. If you find an error, that's a bug we want to fix.

Spoke length

L = √(R² + r² + f² − 2·R·r·cos θ) − ½·(flange spoke-hole dia)

  • R = ERD / 2 — the rim's effective radius, the point the spoke end should reach.
  • r = flange PCD / 2 — radius of the hub's spoke-hole circle.
  • f — axial centre-to-flange run, adjusted by hub offset and by rim offset. Offset / asymmetric rims drill each side's holes off-centre (e.g. 6.3 L, −6.3 R shifts the whole bed sideways; 11 L, 11 R widens it) — picking such a rim fills the per-side drilling offsets straight from the catalogue.
  • θ = crosses × 720 / spoke-count — angle at the axle between a hub hole and its rim hole.
  • We subtract half the hub flange hole (the spoke seats against the far side of the hole). This is a hub dimension — that's why it lives in the Hub panel.

Three choices a careful builder should know about

  • Spoke-stretch compensation (optional). By default we report the geometric length, like spocalc, DT and Sapim. Tick compensate and each spoke is shortened by the elongation it takes at tension — ΔL = T·L/(E·A), with E = 200 GPa and A = π·(centre gauge/2)² (a spoke stretches in its thin centre, so we use the centre gauge). Each side stretches by its own tension share, not the full build tension.
  • Bench measurements beat catalogue specs. Manufacturer ERD especially is often optimistic. When precision matters, measure your own rim (two-spoke method) and hub.

The other numbers

  • Bracing angle = atan(f / in-plane run) — the spoke's angle to the wheel plane; more is better lateral support.
  • Tension distribution — for the wheel to sit centred, T·sin(bracing) must match on both sides, so tension ∝ 1/sin(bracing). We pin the tighter side at 100% and show the other as its true ratio. The familiar "offset ratio" (e.g. 50%) is the small-angle approximation of the same thing.
  • Flange exit angle — how far off radial the spoke leaves the hub; 90° is a perfectly tangential exit.
  • Wrap angle — the angle the spoke makes with the rim, seen from the side. Total angle at rim combines the wrap angle with the bracing angle (edge-on) into the true 3-D misalignment of the spoke with the nipple: acos(cos wrap · cos bracing) — the larger it is, the more the nipple wants a washer or a swivelling seat.

Head clearance (g) — how we compute it

A spoke leaves its flange hole running tangent to a circle of radius p = R·r·sin θ / in-plane (the circle the crossing spokes wrap around). Its nearest crossing neighbour's hole sits one hole over, at flange angle φ = 360 / (spokes on that flange). g is the perpendicular distance from that neighbour's hole to this spoke's straight line:

g = p·(cos φ − 1) + √(r² − p²)·sin φ, then clearance = √(g² + flange_thickness²) − head_dia/2 − elbow_gauge/2

The neighbour's head sits on the far face of the flange (or recessed in its hole), so it's offset from the passing spoke axially by roughly the flange thickness — the real gap is that 3-D distance, less the passing spoke's radius (½ elbow gauge) and the head's radius (½ head dia). Sanity check on g: a radial spoke has p = 0, so g = r·sin φ (just the chord spacing between holes); as the lacing turns tangential p → r and g → r·(cos φ − 1), i.e. the spoke line crosses the neighbour hole, and only the flange thickness keeps the head clear. It's an approximation of a genuinely 3-D interaction, so treat the number as a guide and read the hub-detail diagram (drawn at a shared scale for both flanges) alongside it; for crow's foot it's computed on the crossed spokes.

Lacing patterns

Each spoke's angle at the axle is θ = crosses × 360 / (spokes on that flange). We support radial (0×) through 5-cross (one length), crow's foot — a repeating radial + crossed group that yields two lengths and needs the spokes-per-side divisible by 3 (24/36/48h) — and 2:1 "triplet", where the drive (right) flange carries twice the spokes of the other. In 2:1 each flange uses its own spoke count for θ and for the tension balance (so the heavy side runs slacker — the whole point of the pattern); it needs the total divisible by 6. Honest caveat: real 2:1 spaces the rim holes slightly unevenly, so true lengths can sit a fraction of a millimetre off the even-distribution value we report.

Straight-pull hubs

Set Spoke style → straight-pull and we drop the ½-hole term, since a straight-pull head seats in the flange hole instead of wrapping a J-bend elbow around it. Straight-pull geometry varies by hub, so treat this as first-order and use the maker's own flange figures when you have them.

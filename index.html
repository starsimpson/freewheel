The order line under each length rounds to the nearest mm (and the nearest even length, since many spokes are stocked in 2 mm steps); when in doubt, don't round up past the rim. Head clearance is the 3-D gap from a spoke to its crossing neighbour's elbow, using your flange thickness and spoke gauge — below ~1 mm (shown in red) the heads may foul, so try one cross less.

The math (and what each number means)

Spoke length uses the standard 3-D triangle: L = √(R² + r² + f² − 2·R·r·cos θ) − ½·(spoke hole dia), where R = ERD/2, r = flange PCD/2, f is the axial center-to-flange distance (adjusted by hub offset and drilling offset), and θ = crosses × 720 / spoke-count is the angle subtended at the axle between a hub hole and its rim hole. Bracing angle = atan(f / in-plane run): a bigger angle braces the wheel better laterally. Tension distribution compares the two bracing angles — the shallower (usually drive) side carries the higher tension. Effective tangential PCD is the circle the spokes run tangent to; flange exit and wrap are how far off radial the spoke leaves the hub and meets the rim. Spoke head clearance is modelled as the 3-D distance between a spoke and the elbow of its immediate crossing neighbour: clearance = √(gap² + flange_thickness²) − spoke_gauge, where gap is the in-plane perpendicular distance from the neighbour hole to the spoke line. Because adjacent heads sit on opposite faces of the flange, thickness genuinely buys room — the hub-detail diagram lets you see it directly.

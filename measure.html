Rim ERD — the two-spoke method

ERD (effective rim diameter) is the diameter the spoke ends must reach. It's the single most important — and least reliable — number in the build, so measure it with the nipples you'll actually use.

  1. Take two spokes you don't mind sacrificing and two nipples from your build.
  2. Thread a nipple onto each spoke until the spoke end is flush with the bottom of the screwdriver slot — the point the spoke should reach.
  3. With calipers, measure each spoke from the inside of the elbow to that flush point: S₁ and S₂.
  4. Drop both assemblies into two directly-opposite spoke holes, nipples seated in the rim bed, spokes pointing toward the centre.
  5. Measure the gap between the two elbows across the middle: G. If the spokes overlap, enter G as negative.

ERD = S₁ + S₂ + G — the straight line from one flush point through the centre to the other.

Measure twice. Repeat on a couple of hole-pairs and average — rims are rarely perfectly round.

Hub — flange dimensions

For each flange you need two numbers, plus the over-locknut width.

  1. Flange PCD (pitch-circle diameter): the diameter of the circle the spoke holes sit on. Measure centre-to-centre across two opposite holes; if you can only reach adjacent holes, measure hole spacing and let the geometry give the circle, or measure across the flange and subtract a hole diameter.
  2. Centre-to-flange (left and right): the distance from the hub's centre plane to each flange's spoke-hole face. Measure the over-locknut width (OLN), halve it to find the centre, then measure from a locknut face to each flange and subtract.
  3. OLN: the width across the locknuts — 100 mm front / 130–142 mm rear are typical. It sets where the centre plane is.

Rear and disc hubs are asymmetric — the two centre-to-flange numbers differ, and that difference is your dish. Enter both sides separately in the calculator.

Why bother

Catalogue ERD is often optimistic or rounded, and a millimetre of ERD error is a whole spoke size. When you send a measured ERD from here, the calculator drops its uncertainty band to zero — because now you know the number. Everything stays in your browser.
